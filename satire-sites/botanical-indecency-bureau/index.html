About the Botanical Indecency Bureau

OFFICIAL STATEMENT The Botanical Indecency Bureau (BIB) was established in 1923 under the Public Decency and Floral Conduct Act (49 U.S.C. § 8721-8899) to regulate the display, sale, and distribution of botanically explicit plant matter in public spaces. All flowering plants are, by biological definition, the reproductive organs of plants—a reality that demands careful government oversight.

Statutory Authority

The Bureau operates under Title 49, Subtitle D, Chapter 8721 (The Public Floral Decency Protection Act of 1923, as amended) and enforces compliance through:

  • Classification Standards: Determination of botanical explicitness levels
  • Display Regulations: Rules governing public exhibition of reproductive organs
  • Sale Restrictions: Age verification for high-risk floral purchases
  • Distribution Controls: Licensing requirements for florists and garden centers
  • Enforcement Actions: Citations, fines, and temporary business closures

Our Authority

As of 2024, the Botanical Indecency Bureau maintains regulatory jurisdiction over all 50 states, US territories, and federal lands. Field agents operate from regional offices in every major metropolitan area. The Bureau's annual budget of $487 million funds enforcement operations, public education campaigns, and botanical research initiatives.

Our Mission

"To safeguard the American public from unauthorized exposure to botanical reproduction, ensuring that all floral materials are properly classified, labeled, and displayed with appropriate content warnings. The BIB is committed to reducing childhood exposure to explicit stamens, mitigating pollen-related health risks, and maintaining botanical decency standards consistent with community values."

Core Principles

Transparency: All botanical products must clearly disclose their reproductive status and explicitly visible anatomical structures.

Age-Appropriateness: The public display and sale of botanically explicit flowers to minors is strictly regulated. Orchids, lilies, and other Class III-IV specimens require age verification (18+ in most jurisdictions).

Public Health: High-pollen flowers (roses, lilies, sunflowers) pose genuine respiratory hazards. The BIB monitors pollen counts and issues seasonal advisories.

Community Standards: Local BIB field offices work with municipalities to enforce regulations consistent with regional comfort levels regarding botanical exposure.

Historical Context

The Bureau was founded in 1923 following the "Tulip Crisis of 1922," when uncovered tulip bulbs in a Philadelphia warehouse resulted in widespread pollen exposure affecting over 40,000 residents. This watershed moment prompted Congress to act, establishing regulatory oversight of botanically reproductive materials. Since then, the Bureau has evolved to address emerging floral threats, including hybrid ornamentals, rare tropical species, and internet-based seed distribution networks.

Violation Classification System

The Bureau categorizes botanical specimens into four classes based on reproductive explicitness, anatomical visibility, and potential for indecent exposure. Each class carries distinct regulatory requirements and violation penalties.

CLASS I — MILD INDECENCY

Definition: Flowers with minimal visible reproductive structures; stamens and pistils largely enclosed within sepals or petals.

Explicitness Level: Low (10-25% visible anatomical exposure)

Examples: Daisies, sunflowers, carnations, marigolds, zinnias

Regulatory Requirements:
  • Basic age restriction (sale to 12+)
  • Point-of-sale disclosure label required
  • Public display must include warning placard
  • Pollen containment measures recommended

CLASS II — MODERATE INDECENCY

Definition: Flowers with partially exposed stamens and pistils; moderate anatomical detail visible to casual observers.

Explicitness Level: Moderate (25-60% visible anatomical exposure)

Examples: Tulips, poppies, hibiscus, peonies, anemones

Regulatory Requirements:
  • Age restriction for purchase (sale to 14+)
  • Prominent warning labels on all stems
  • Mandatory pollen shields on public display
  • Florist training certification required
  • Distance restrictions near schools/libraries

CLASS III — EXPLICIT INDECENCY

Definition: Flowers with highly exposed reproductive organs; stamens and pistils prominently displayed with minimal botanical clothing.

Explicitness Level: High (60-85% visible anatomical exposure)

Examples: Lilies, gladiolus, zinnias, dahlias, proteas

Regulatory Requirements:
  • Strict age verification (18+ only)
  • Sealed/opaque packaging required
  • Public display strictly prohibited
  • Pollen extraction or containment mandatory
  • Licensed botanical distributor status required
  • Monthly compliance inspections

CLASS IV — EXTREME INDECENCY

Definition: Flowers of maximum botanical explicitness with fully exposed reproductive organs; no petal coverage; flagrant anatomical display.

Explicitness Level: Extreme (85-100% visible anatomical exposure)

Examples: Orchids, anthuriums, specific tropical hybrids, experimental cultivars

Regulatory Requirements:
  • Federal license required for possession
  • Strict containment protocols mandated
  • Public display entirely prohibited
  • Interstate transport requires permits
  • Quarterly federal inspection required
  • Special pollen hazmat training mandated
  • Potential criminal charges for violations

Classification Disputes

Proprietors who believe their botanical materials have been incorrectly classified may file an appeal with the regional BIB office within 30 days. Reclassification requests trigger a formal botanical review process involving certified florists and government botanists. The typical appeal resolution takes 45-90 days.

Penalties

First Offense: Warning and remediation requirement

Public Advisories & Seasonal Warnings

SPRING 2024 BOTANICAL ALERT

Pollen levels are currently ELEVATED across most of the continental United States. Spring bloom season brings significantly increased reproductive activity among Class II and III specimens. The Bureau recommends:

  • Exercise caution when approaching public gardens and parks
  • Wear respiratory protection in high-pollen areas
  • Avoid purchasing unpackaged flowers during peak bloom season
  • Monitor local BIB advisories for specific botanical species warnings
ELEVATED
TROPICAL ORCHID IMPORT ADVISORY

The Bureau has identified a recent surge in unregulated Class IV orchid imports from Southeast Asian suppliers. These specimens have been detected in garden centers, flower shops, and online retailers without proper federal licensing documentation. Consumers are advised that unauthorized possession of Class IV specimens is a federal violation. Report suspicious orchid sales to the BIB immediately.

CRITICAL
WEDDING SEASON COMPLIANCE REMINDER

With wedding season approaching, the Bureau reminds florists and event planners that floral arrangements visible in public spaces (ceremony venues with windows, outdoor receptions, parking lot displays) must comply with all classification regulations. Class III and IV specimens are strictly prohibited in any publicly visible installation. Violators face significant fines and potential business licensing suspension.

ELEVATED
ONLINE SEED VENDOR ALERT

Multiple unregulated online retailers are currently selling Class II and III seeds without proper age verification gateways. The Bureau is conducting a federal investigation. Consumers who have received unpackaged seeds from vendors without official BIB licensing should file a report immediately. This is a matter of critical regulatory importance.
